EV Charger Installation Guide

What you actually need to know before installing a home charger

Home EV Charging Installation

Installing a home charger involves more than just adding an outlet. We'll assess your electrical capacity, handle permits, and ensure safe, code-compliant installation. Here's what goes into the process.

Level 1 vs Level 2 Charging

Level 1 Charging

Standard 120V outlet. Adds about 4 miles of range per hour. Works well for plug-in hybrids or if you drive under 40 miles daily. No installation needed - just plug in.

Level 2 Charging

240V circuit (like your dryer uses). Adds 25-40 miles per hour depending on your vehicle and charger. This is what most EV owners install for overnight charging.

Hardwired vs Plug-in

Hardwired: Permanent installation, cleaner appearance, required for outdoor locations
Plug-in: Uses a NEMA 14-50 outlet, portable if you move, can swap chargers easily

Electrical Requirements

Panel Capacity

EV chargers typically require 40-60 amps. Your current electrical load determines if an upgrade is needed:

  • 100-amp panel: Often at capacity with modern appliances. We'll calculate your actual load to determine options.
  • 200-amp panel: Usually has capacity for EV charging, but load calculation confirms this.
  • Wire run distance: Longer runs from panel to parking area increase installation cost.

Installation Cost Factors

Straightforward installation: Panel in garage with available capacity: $800-1,200

Standard installation: Panel in basement, attached garage: $1,500-2,500

Complex installation: 100-amp panel requiring upgrade, detached garage: $3,000-5,000+

Vehicle-Specific Considerations

Tesla Owners

  • Tesla Wall Connector can share power with multiple Teslas
  • Mobile connector works fine for most daily charging
  • Wall Connector looks cleaner but costs more

Ford F-150 Lightning

  • Ford Charge Station Pro enables home backup power
  • Requires additional equipment for home backup feature
  • 80-amp charging for faster fill-ups on that huge battery

Chevy Bolt/Volt

  • Smaller batteries charge fine on 32-amp circuits
  • Dual-voltage charger works on 120V or 240V
  • Great for saving money on installation

The Permit Question

Yes, you need a permit in Washtenaw County. Here's why it's worth doing right:

  • Insurance may deny claims for unpermitted work
  • You'll need it when you sell your house
  • Inspection ensures it's safe (240V can kill)
  • We handle all permit paperwork

DTE Energy Rebates & Rates

Rebate: DTE offers $500 rebate for Level 2 charger installation (check current program status)

Time-of-Use Rates: Charge overnight for roughly half the cost of daytime rates

Separate Meter: Not usually worth it for residential

Installation Timeline

  1. Assessment: We check your panel and measure the wire run
  2. Quote: Fixed price with everything included
  3. Permit: We submit, usually approved in 3-5 days
  4. Installation: Half day to full day depending on complexity
  5. Inspection: City inspects, you start charging!

Money-Saving Tips

  • NEMA 14-50 outlet is usually cheaper than hardwired
  • 32-amp circuit is enough for most drivers (vs 40-48 amp)
  • Indoor installations cost less than outdoor
  • Combine with panel upgrade if you need one anyway

Questions We Always Get

Q: Can I use my dryer outlet?
A: Sometimes. If it's in the garage and you don't mind unplugging to do laundry.

Q: What if I move?
A: Plug-in chargers can go with you. Hardwired adds value to your home.

Q: How fast will it charge?
A: Most EVs gain 25-30 miles per hour on a 40-amp circuit. Overnight is plenty for daily driving.
